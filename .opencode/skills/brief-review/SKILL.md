# Brief Review & Improvement

**Before reviewing any brief, read:**
1. `/mnt/skills/public/blink-brand-context/SKILL.md` — brand voice, product facts, messaging rules
2. `/mnt/skills/public/content-briefs/SKILL.md` — structural standards and anatomy

---

## What This Skill Does

Given a content brief (draft, existing, or submitted), this skill:
1. **Evaluates** it against a rubric — story, audience, hooks, structure, tone, campaign focus
2. **Scores** it on each dimension with evidence
3. **Identifies** the highest-leverage weaknesses
4. **Rewrites or improves** the weak sections (or the full brief, if needed)

You can be asked to do any part of this in isolation — just a score, just feedback, just a rewrite of one section. Default to full evaluation + top 3 improvement recommendations unless instructed otherwise.

---

## Step 1: Identify the Campaign Focus

Before evaluating anything, identify:

**What is this brief's specific campaign focus?**
- Is it tied to a specific use case (website builder, AI apps, mobile, etc.) or is it general?
- Who is the target creator / audience?
- What's the intended platform (TikTok, Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts, LinkedIn)?

A campaign-focused brief should be evaluated *on its own terms* — it doesn't need to cover all of Blink's features. It needs to tell *one great story* about *one specific thing Blink does* for *one specific audience*. Penalize a brief for trying to cover everything. Reward sharp focus.

If the campaign focus is unclear from the brief itself, flag this as a critical failure — a brief without a clear angle cannot be evaluated or fixed until the angle is defined.

---

## Step 2: The Evaluation Rubric

Score each dimension 1–5. Use the criteria below. Don't round up — be honest.

---

### Dimension 1: The Core Story (Weight: High)
*Does the brief have one clear, emotionally resonant narrative arc?*

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | One unmistakable story. A stranger could read just the Core Story section and know exactly what this video is about and why it matters. The pain point is quoted in the audience's voice. The "one thing Blink does" is specific and non-generic. |
| 4 | Strong story with minor gaps — either the audience isn't precise enough, or the pain point feels assumed rather than felt, or the one thing Blink does is slightly generic. |
| 3 | A story exists but competes with too many other ideas. The brief is trying to say 2–3 things instead of one. |
| 2 | The campaign overview reads like a product summary, not a story. Features are present. Narrative is not. |
| 1 | No discernible narrative. The brief is a list of talking points dressed up as a campaign. |

**Key test:** Ask "what is this video about?" after reading the Core Story section. If the answer takes more than one sentence, score ≤ 3.

---

### Dimension 2: Audience Precision (Weight: High)
*Does the brief define a specific person with a specific pain, or a demographic abstraction?*

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Named personas with lived pain points quoted in their own voice (e.g., "I know what I want my website to look like. I've seen designs I love. But I can't build them myself."). Audience could be described to a stranger and they'd picture a real person. |
| 4 | Specific personas but pain points are paraphrased rather than felt. |
| 3 | Audience is a category ("entrepreneurs", "content creators") with some qualification. Pain points are functional, not emotional. |
| 2 | Audience is defined by demographics only. No pain point. |
| 1 | No audience definition. |

---

### Dimension 3: Hook Quality (Weight: High)
*Are the hook options specific, usable, and Blink-native?*

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | 10+ hooks. Each one is immediately usable as-is — no creator would need to edit it first. Hooks vary in angle and tone. At least half contain a specific dollar amount, time reference, or named competitor contrast. None could be used for a different product without modification. |
| 4 | Strong hooks, but some are too generic or interchangeable with other SaaS tools. |
| 3 | Hooks exist but most feel templated ("POV: you want to build an app..."). Missing the specificity that makes them scroll-stopping. |
| 2 | Hooks are present but feel like placeholders. Creator would need to rewrite them all. |
| 1 | No hooks, or hooks are just feature descriptions. |

**Key test:** Take the weakest hook. Could it be used by Cursor, Webflow, or Shopify with minor edits? If yes, score ≤ 3.

---

### Dimension 4: Campaign Focus Discipline (Weight: High)
*Does the brief stay on its specific angle, or does it try to cover all of Blink?*

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Every section serves the single campaign narrative. Talking points are filtered to what's relevant to this angle. Features not relevant to this use case are omitted. |
| 4 | Mostly focused, with 1–2 talking points that drift into general product territory. |
| 3 | The campaign angle is present but shares real estate with general Blink features. Feels like a general brief with a campaign wrapper. |
| 2 | Campaign focus exists only in the title. The body is a general product brief. |
| 1 | No campaign focus. Everything is on the page. |

---

### Dimension 5: Talking Points as Value Frames (Weight: Medium)
*Are talking points "why should I care" statements, or just feature descriptions?*

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Every talking point answers "so what?" explicitly. A creator reading them gets not just what Blink does but why their audience will respond to it. The framing is audience-first. |
| 4 | Most talking points are well-framed. 1–2 still read as feature descriptions. |
| 3 | Mixed. Some talking points are audience-framed, others are product-framed. |
| 2 | Talking points are a feature list with headers. |
| 1 | No talking points, or they're raw spec. |

---

### Dimension 6: Reel Flow Believability (Weight: Medium)
*Does the sample reel flow describe a real video or a corporate storyboard?*

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Timestamped, specific, and cinematic. A creator could shoot from this. The flow builds tension (result first, reveal second) and the cost comparison moment lands with weight. Includes a clear note that it's suggestive, not prescriptive. |
| 4 | Strong structure, but some moments feel generic or the timing is off. |
| 3 | Structure is correct but reads as an outline, not a video. Missing the visual and emotional specificity. |
| 2 | Reel flow is present but feels like a marketing deck slide. |
| 1 | No reel flow. |

---

### Dimension 7: Creator Freedom vs. Over-Specification (Weight: Medium)
*Does the brief give direction without killing creative voice?*

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Direction is on outcome and emotion, not execution. Creators have multiple hook options, multiple reel structures to choose from. The brief communicates trust. Creator Notes explains the *why* so creators can make smart choices independently. |
| 4 | Mostly good, with 1–2 moments of over-prescription. |
| 3 | Brief is specific enough that creators might feel constrained, or loose enough that they'd make off-brand choices. |
| 2 | Brief reads as a script the creator should follow, not a direction they should interpret. |
| 1 | Prescriptive to the point of producing ad content. |

---

### Dimension 8: Brand Voice Compliance (Weight: Medium)
*Does the brief's own language model the voice Blink wants creators to use?*

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | The brief itself sounds like Blink — inspiring, direct, real, no buzzwords. Reading it makes you want to make the content. |
| 4 | Mostly on-voice with a few corporate phrases. |
| 3 | Some sections read like a marketing brief from 2015. |
| 2 | The brief is formal and corporate. It doesn't model the voice it's asking for. |
| 1 | Brand voice violations throughout. |

**Flag immediately if found:** "game-changing", "disruptive", "leverage", "seamless", "revolutionary", "unlock your potential", "synergy"

---

### Dimension 9: Dos/Don'ts Actionability (Weight: Low)
*Are the rules specific enough to prevent actual mistakes?*

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Every item is a specific, observable behavior. A creator could read it and immediately know what to do or not do without further clarification. |
| 3 | Mix of specific and vague guidance. "Be authentic" appears somewhere. |
| 1 | Vague guidance that a creator could technically comply with while still making bad content. |

---

### Dimension 10: Completeness (Weight: Low)
*Are all required sections present?*

Check for: About Blink, Core Story, Talking Points, Hook Options, Reel Flow, Creator Notes, Dos/Don'ts, Getting Started.

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | All sections present and substantive. |
| 3 | 1–2 sections missing or thin. |
| 1 | Major sections absent. |

## Step 3: Score Summary

After scoring each dimension, produce a summary table:

```
BRIEF REVIEW: [Brief Name]
Campaign Focus: [identified focus or "UNCLEAR"]

Dimension                          | Score | Weight
-----------------------------------|-------|-------
Core Story                         |  X/5  | High
Audience Precision                 |  X/5  | High
Hook Quality                       |  X/5  | High
Campaign Focus Discipline          |  X/5  | High
Talking Points as Value Frames     |  X/5  | Medium
Reel Flow Believability            |  X/5  | Medium
Creator Freedom vs Over-Specify    |  X/5  | Medium
Brand Voice Compliance             |  X/5  | Medium
Dos/Don'ts Actionability           |  X/5  | Low
Completeness                       |  X/5  | Low

WEIGHTED SCORE: XX/50
OVERALL GRADE: [Strong / Good / Needs Work / Significant Revision / Start Over]
```

Grade thresholds:
- **Strong (45–50):** Ready to send. Minor polish only.
- **Good (37–44):** Send after addressing top 2–3 issues.
- **Needs Work (27–36):** Significant sections need rewriting before sending.
- **Significant Revision (17–26):** Core story and/or hooks need a rebuild.
- **Start Over (<17):** The brief's foundation (story + audience) isn't there. Easier to restart than patch.

---

## Step 4: Prioritized Feedback

After the score table, provide exactly **3 prioritized improvement recommendations** in this format:

**#1 [Dimension Name] — [One-line diagnosis]**
What's wrong: [1–2 sentences of specific evidence from the brief]
Why it matters: [What this failure costs in creator output]
How to fix it: [Specific, actionable direction — not "improve the hooks" but "rewrite hooks 3, 7, and 11 to include a dollar amount and a specific audience scenario"]

Repeat for #2 and #3.

If the brief scores Strong, acknowledge what's working and note any minor refinements.

---

## Step 5: Rewrite (If Requested)

When asked to improve or rewrite, follow this decision tree:

**If score ≥ 37 (Good):** Targeted rewrite — fix only the sections that scored ≤ 3. Leave strong sections intact. Label what changed and why.

**If score 27–36 (Needs Work):** Rebuild the weak sections. Start from Core Story and Hooks if those scored low — everything downstream depends on them. Present the rewritten sections for approval before touching other parts.

**If score < 27 (Significant Revision or Start Over):** Don't patch. Go back to first principles:
1. Ask: what is the one story this campaign needs to tell?
2. Ask: who specifically is the audience and what do they say to themselves?
3. Draft the Core Story and 5 hooks first. Get those approved.
4. Then build the rest of the brief around them.

---

## Rewrite Principles

When rewriting any section of a brief:

**Story before structure.** If the core narrative is weak, fixing the hooks won't save it. Always fix story problems first.

**Campaign angle is a constraint, not a limitation.** A brief about Blink's AI app features should not mention the website builder even if it's impressive. Discipline makes briefs better. Enforce it.

**Kill generic talking points.** If a talking point could describe Cursor, Webflow, Notion, or any other tool, rewrite it. Every talking point should be Blink-specific and audience-specific.

**Hooks should create a gap.** The best hooks create cognitive dissonance: something that looks expensive was cheap, something that looks complex was instant, something that looks like a team was one person. If a hook doesn't create a gap, it's not a hook.

**Creator Notes is your secret weapon.** Creators who understand the psychology make better content than creators who follow instructions. If the Creator Notes section is thin or absent, write it like you're briefing a smart friend — explain the bet you're making with this narrative and why it works.

**Speak like a human.** Read every sentence aloud. If it sounds like it was written by a committee, rewrite it. If it contains "leverage", "seamless", "unlock", or "disruptive", delete it.

## Campaign-Specific Rewrite Notes

Different campaign focuses require different emphasis. Adjust accordingly:

**Website Builder / Design-focused campaigns**
- Core story centers on the perception gap (looks $5K, costs $20)
- Audience: freelancers, portfolio builders, small business owners who've been priced out of design
- Best hooks: dollar comparisons, agency price reveals, compliment stories
- Demo apps: portfolio sites, landing pages, business sites built from screenshots or sketches

**AI App / Native AI-focused campaigns**
- Core story centers on access to AI capabilities without technical knowledge
- Audience: non-technical builders who want AI in their products
- Best hooks: "I built an AI [X] without writing a single line of code"
- Demo apps: AI voice notes, AI image tools, AI content tools — show the AI doing something visually impressive

**Full-Stack / Production-Ready campaigns**
- Core story centers on the completeness gap (other tools give you a prototype; Blink gives you a product)
- Audience: developers who want to ship faster, non-technical founders who need real infrastructure
- Best hooks: contrast with other tools, show the backend features other tools skip
- Demo apps: apps with auth flows, database-backed features, real user functionality

**Mobile App campaigns**
- Core story centers on democratizing mobile development (previously gated behind iOS/Android skills)
- Audience: people with app ideas who assumed they needed to hire a developer
- Best hooks: "I have an app in the App Store and I don't code"
- Demo apps: utility apps, consumer apps, tools with clear mobile-native use cases

---

## Output Format

When reviewing a brief, produce output in this order:
1. Campaign Focus identification (2–3 sentences)
2. Score Summary table
3. Overall Grade with one-sentence rationale
4. Prioritized Feedback (#1, #2, #3)
5. Rewritten sections (if requested), clearly labeled

Keep the diagnosis tight. A great review is specific and actionable — not comprehensive. The goal is to give whoever reads it exactly what they need to make the brief better, nothing more.
