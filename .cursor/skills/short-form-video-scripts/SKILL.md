## Two Modes

This skill handles two distinct jobs:

**Mode A: Generate** — Write a script from a brief or prompt
**Mode B: Review** — Evaluate and score a script submitted by an influencer

Identify which mode is needed before proceeding. If given a script to evaluate, go to Mode B. If asked to write a script, go to Mode A.

# MODE A: SCRIPT GENERATION

## What Makes a Great Short-Form Script

Short-form video is not a mini-commercial. It's a piece of content that happens to mention a product. The best scripts feel like the creator had something genuinely interesting to share — and Blink is the thing that made it possible.

**The three jobs of a great script:**
1. **Stop the scroll** — the first 3 seconds determine everything
2. **Build desire** — show something the viewer wants before they know they can have it
3. **Lower the barrier** — make trying Blink feel like the obvious next move, not a sales decision

**The core structure that works:**
Result → Hook → Process → Proof → CTA

Never lead with the product. Always lead with the result.

---

## Script Formats by Length

### Format 1: The Reveal (30–45 sec) — Best for broad reach
The workhorse format. Show something impressive, reveal it was AI, show how fast/cheap, CTA.

```
[0–5s]   VISUAL: Show the finished app/site scrolling. No talking yet.
         OVERLAY: Let it breathe. Make it look expensive.
[5–10s]  HOOK: Deliver the hook verbally + text overlay simultaneously.
         e.g. "I just built this for $18. An agency quoted me $6,000."
[10–25s] BUILD: Show the actual Blink build in fast-forward or jump cuts.
         Narrate 1–2 key moments: "I just described what I wanted..."
         "...and it built the whole backend automatically."
[25–35s] PROOF POINT: One specific feature that looks impressive.
         Auth, animations, mobile responsiveness, AI feature — pick one.
[35–45s] CTA: "Blink.new — link in bio. First app is free."
```

**When to use:** Cold audience, broad demographic, product awareness goal.

---

### Format 2: The Problem-Solution (45–60 sec) — Best for high-intent audiences
Open with the pain. Build empathy. Then introduce Blink as the inevitable answer.

```
[0–8s]   PROBLEM: Describe the pain in the audience's voice.
         "Every time I needed a website I had two options: pay thousands,
         or spend weeks learning Webflow and still end up with something ugly."
[8–15s]  AGITATION: Make the pain feel real and shared.
         "I've wasted so much time on tools that promise everything
         and still leave you stuck on the backend."
[15–20s] PIVOT: Natural transition. "Then I found Blink."
         Don't oversell the pivot. Keep it casual.
[20–45s] DEMONSTRATION: Show the build. Narrate the key moments.
         Focus on what was previously hard that is now effortless.
[45–55s] RESULT: Return to the finished product. Let it land.
         "This is what I shipped. Took me 25 minutes."
[55–60s] CTA: "Blink.new — try it free."
```

**When to use:** Creator has an audience with a known, specific pain point. Works especially well for freelancers, developers, solopreneurs.

---

### Format 3: The Story (60–90 sec) — Best for high engagement, saves/shares
Built around a personal narrative. The product is the turning point in a story, not the subject of it.

```
[0–10s]  HOOK: Tease the payoff. "I just got my first client referral from
         someone who thought I had a whole design team."
[10–25s] BACKSTORY: Set the scene. Who are you, what were you trying to do,
         what was standing in your way?
[25–40s] THE MOMENT: When you found / decided to try Blink.
         Keep this brief. Don't linger on the product discovery.
[40–65s] THE BUILD + RESULT: Show what you made. Narrate the impressive parts.
         Weave in 2–3 Blink-specific moments naturally.
[65–80s] THE PAYOFF: The human outcome — client reaction, income, response,
         or personal feeling. This is the emotional peak of the video.
[80–90s] CTA: Soft and earned. "Blink.new — link in bio if you want to try it."
```

**When to use:** Creator has storytelling skills and an engaged audience. Generates saves, comments, and shares more than views.

---

### Format 4: The Comparison (30–45 sec) — Best for converting aware audiences
Side-by-side or contrast structure. Works when the audience already knows the pain.

```
[0–5s]   SETUP: "Here's what this would cost the normal way."
         Show a quote, a price, a timeline — something concrete.
[5–20s]  THE NORMAL WAY: Briefly acknowledge the traditional path.
         Don't spend too long here. Just enough to make the contrast real.
[20–35s] THE BLINK WAY: Same output. Different cost/time. Show the result.
[35–45s] LANDING: Let the numbers speak. Don't editorialize.
         "Agency: $8,000 and 6 weeks. Blink: $20 and 40 minutes."
         CTA: "Blink.new"
```

**When to use:** Tech-aware audience, developer community, people who've been burned by alternatives.

## Script Generation Process

When asked to generate a script:

### Step 1: Identify inputs
Collect (or infer from the brief):
- Campaign focus / use case
- Creator's audience and tone (casual, technical, humorous, professional)
- Desired length (15s / 30s / 45s / 60s / 90s)
- Demo app being used (or suggest one if not specified)
- Platform (TikTok, Reels, YouTube Shorts — affects pacing and text overlay usage)

### Step 2: Choose the right format
Match format to goal:
- Awareness / reach → Format 1: The Reveal
- High-intent / niche audience → Format 2: Problem-Solution
- Engagement / community → Format 3: The Story
- Conversion / comparison shopping → Format 4: The Comparison

If unsure, default to Format 1. It's the most versatile.

### Step 3: Write the hook first
Don't write the script top-to-bottom. Write the hook first and get it right. The rest of the script is in service of that hook.

A good hook has one of these structures:
- **The price reveal:** "I just built [X] for $[amount]. [Category] charges $[much more]."
- **The POV:** "POV: You need [thing] but don't have [resource/skill]..."
- **The challenge:** "Stop [common behavior]. Do this instead."
- **The compliment trap:** "My client asked for my designer's contact. I don't have one."
- **The impossibility:** "I built an app that [impressive thing] without writing a single line of code."
- **The time reveal:** "I built this in [surprisingly short time]. It took [comparison] weeks."

### Step 4: Write the full script
Format: alternating VISUAL and AUDIO blocks with timestamps.

```
[0–5s]
VISUAL: [What the viewer sees — be specific]
AUDIO: [What the creator says — write actual words, not descriptions]
OVERLAY: [Text overlaid on screen, if any]

[5–15s]
VISUAL: ...
AUDIO: ...
OVERLAY: ...
```

Be specific. Don't write "show building the app" — write "screen recording, fast-forward 8x, Blink interface building the dashboard component, cursor moving fast." Don't write "explain the features" — write the actual words.

### Step 5: Annotate for the creator
After the script, add a brief DIRECTOR'S NOTE (3–5 sentences) explaining:
- Why this hook was chosen
- What the emotional arc is trying to do
- One thing to absolutely nail in the execution
- One thing to avoid

This replaces a lengthy briefing conversation and helps the creator make the script their own.

---

## Generation Quality Checks

Before finalizing a script, verify:
- [ ] First 3 seconds would stop a scroll — be honest
- [ ] Product is not mentioned until after desire is established
- [ ] At least one specific dollar amount or time reference
- [ ] Script reads naturally aloud — no sentence sounds like marketing copy
- [ ] The demo app shown is impressive and believable for this audience
- [ ] CTA is soft and earned, not a hard sell
- [ ] Nothing in the script could only work as a paid ad — it should feel creator-native

# MODE B: SCRIPT REVIEW

## Philosophy

Reviewing a creator's script is a creative collaboration, not a compliance check. The goal is to make the script perform better while keeping the creator's voice intact. You're not rewriting it in Blink's voice — you're helping it work harder in their voice.

That said: standards exist. A script that violates core principles (leads with the product, buries the result, feels like an ad) needs to be flagged clearly and fixed, regardless of the creator's preferences.

---

## The Review Rubric

Score each dimension 1–5.

---

### Dimension 1: The Hook (Weight: Critical)
*Does the first 3 seconds demand attention?*

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Immediately compelling. Creates a gap (expectation vs. reality) or triggers curiosity that can only be resolved by watching. Specific — not generic. |
| 4 | Strong but slightly generic. Would work but could be sharper. |
| 3 | Functional but forgettable. Wouldn't stop a scroll in a competitive feed. |
| 2 | Opens with product name or a description of what the video is about. |
| 1 | No hook. Opens with greeting, introduction, or "so today I'm going to show you..." |

**Auto-fail:** Script opens with "Hey guys", product name in first sentence, or any variation of "Today I want to talk about Blink."

---

### Dimension 2: Result-First Structure (Weight: Critical)
*Is the impressive output shown before the method is revealed?*

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Finished result shown/described first, always. Viewer sees the "what" before the "how." |
| 4 | Mostly result-first with a brief setup that's earned. |
| 3 | Some tension between showing result and explaining product. |
| 2 | Product is introduced before the result is established. |
| 1 | Script is structured as a product demo: feature → feature → feature → result. |

---

### Dimension 3: Creator Voice (Weight: High)
*Does the script sound like this specific creator, or like a Blink marketing template?*

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Unmistakably this creator's voice. Someone who follows them would recognize their style immediately. Blink fits naturally into their content ecosystem. |
| 4 | Mostly creator-native with 1–2 moments that feel like borrowed marketing language. |
| 3 | Half creator voice, half marketing copy. Viewers would sense it's sponsored. |
| 2 | The script is a Blink script with the creator's name on it. |
| 1 | Pure ad copy. No creator voice present. |

---

### Dimension 4: The Desire Gap (Weight: High)
*Does the script create want before it creates understanding?*

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Viewer thinks "I want that" before they know what Blink is. The product is the answer to a desire that's already been established. |
| 4 | Desire is established early but could be held longer before the reveal. |
| 3 | Desire and product explanation happen simultaneously. |
| 2 | Script explains the product, then tries to create desire. Wrong order. |
| 1 | No desire created. Script is entirely explanatory. |

---

### Dimension 5: Specificity (Weight: High)
*Are there real numbers, real scenarios, real details — or vague claims?*

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | At least one specific dollar amount. At least one specific time reference. The demo app is named and described specifically. Feels real and verifiable. |
| 4 | Good specificity in some areas, vague in others. |
| 3 | One specific detail in an otherwise vague script. |
| 2 | All claims are vague: "so much cheaper", "really fast", "really impressive." |
| 1 | No specificity. Script could describe any product in any category. |

---

### Dimension 6: The Cost/Value Moment (Weight: Medium)
*Does the script land a clear, visceral cost comparison?*

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Cost comparison is a distinct beat that lands with weight. Hard numbers on both sides. The gap is the point — don't rush past it. |
| 4 | Cost comparison is present but undersold. |
| 3 | Vague cost reference ("way cheaper than hiring someone"). |
| 2 | No cost comparison, but the script works without it. |
| 1 | No cost comparison and the script feels incomplete because of it. |

---

### Dimension 7: Brand Compliance (Weight: Medium)
*Does the script follow Blink's brand rules?*

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Blink referred to correctly (Blink.new or Blink). No banned language. Nothing that would embarrass the brand. CTA included. Comments/likes not restricted. |
| 3 | Minor compliance issues (wrong brand name format, missing CTA). |
| 1 | Brand name wrong, major compliance violation, or content makes a false claim. |

**Flag immediately:**
- Brand called "BLINK", "blink", or "blinkdotnew"
- Claims that aren't in blink-brand-context (don't make up features or pricing)
- Content that feels deceptive (hiding sponsorship, fake reactions)

---

### Dimension 8: CTA Quality (Weight: Low)
*Is the call to action earned and frictionless?*

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | CTA feels like a natural conclusion. Low-friction ("try it free", "link in bio"). Not a hard sell. |
| 3 | CTA is present but feels bolted on. |
| 1 | No CTA, or CTA is aggressive/salesy. |

---

## Score Summary Format

```
SCRIPT REVIEW: [Creator Name / Script Title]
Platform: [TikTok / Reels / Shorts]
Length: [X seconds]

Dimension                    | Score | Weight
-----------------------------|-------|--------
Hook                         |  X/5  | Critical
Result-First Structure       |  X/5  | Critical
Creator Voice                |  X/5  | High
Desire Gap                   |  X/5  | High
Specificity                  |  X/5  | High
Cost/Value Moment            |  X/5  | Medium
Brand Compliance             |  X/5  | Medium
CTA Quality                  |  X/5  | Low

TOTAL: XX/40
GRADE: [Approve / Approve with Notes / Revise / Reject]
```

Grade thresholds:
- **Approve (36–40):** Send it. Script is strong.
- **Approve with Notes (28–35):** Good enough to shoot. Address notes before or during edit.
- **Revise (18–27):** Core issues need fixing before production. Return to creator with specific feedback.
- **Reject (<18):** Script needs a rebuild. Auto-fail on Hook or Result-First Structure also triggers Reject regardless of total score.

---

## Review Feedback Format

After the score table, provide:

**What's working** (2–3 sentences) — Be specific. This preserves the creator's confidence and tells them what not to change.

**Must fix before shooting** — Only the issues that would materially hurt performance. Max 3. Each item:
- The problem (specific quote from the script if possible)
- Why it matters
- How to fix it (concrete suggestion, not "make it better")

**Nice to have** — Optional improvements that would elevate the script but aren't blocking. Keep this short — 1–2 items max. Don't overwhelm.

---

## Tone When Reviewing

Creators are partners, not employees. Feedback should feel like a smart friend who's seen a lot of these videos, not a compliance officer checking a form.

- Lead with what's working
- Be direct about what isn't — don't soften problems into non-feedback
- When something needs to change, say what it should become, not just what it is
- Never rewrite their voice. Suggest, don't replace.
- If a script is genuinely strong, say so clearly. Don't invent problems.
